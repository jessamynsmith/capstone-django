About the data:

The spatial database for the Global Inland Water Explorer was derived from the NASA MODIS Water 250 m Mask. The water mask, which consists of 15 years of global inland surface water plus auxiliary Quality Assurance layers in raster format, is delivered in 291 tiles for each year 2000 – 2015.

Methods:

To develop the database, the initial tile rasters were stitched together to create annual global water mask mosaics. A layer (Max Extent) was created to represent the maximum extent for each water body among all years. Oceans were removed and some large water bodies were artificially detached from one another so that they accurately represented separate objects in the database. A spatial join between the Max Extent layer and each annual mosaic rasters was performed so that the Max Extent’s common ID could be shared between overlapping inland water bodies across time.

The resulting Max Extent layer and annual layers were then converted to polygon shapefiles. Surface area (km2) was calculated for all objects in all shapefiles. In turn, the tables for these annual layers were joined to the Max Extent layer so that it would include annual area for each water body.

A linear trend time-series analysis was run on the annual areas to obtain information such as rate of change (km2/year), p-value, and R2, all of which were stored as attributes. To provide geographical context, the MaxExtent shapefile was then spatially joined with the Global Lakes and Wetlands Dataset (GLWD), a static database of large water bodies plus attributes such as lake name, water body type, and more. Additionally, a countries shapefile with continent, region, subregion attributes was spatially joined to MaxExtent to obtain more context for those water bodies that were not included in the GLWD.

Finally, the resulting MaxExtent layer shapefile plus attributes was imported to a PostgreSQL/PostGIS spatial database. This database is the driving force behind the Global Inland Water Explorer.

  • Importing each annual layer into spatial databases so that the geometry for each water body can be visualized over time
